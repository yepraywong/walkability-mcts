Integrating Walkability Maps for Fall-Preventive Social Navigation

Xiangrui Wang, Nathaniel Pearson, Boyi Hu, Yu Gu
Interactive Robotics Laboratory, West Virginia University
Paper Code arXiv

Walkability-aware social navigation. To reduce human fall risks, the robot utilizes its knowledge of the walkability map to guide pedestrians away from slippery areas. The planned robot walkability-aware trajectory shifts the pedestrian's path from their baseline trajectory in slippery areas (Orange) to the influenced trajectory, ensuring movement through safe, high-walkability regions (Green).

Abstract

Falls are a major accident risk in retail stores, where customers often overlook floor hazards. While robots are commonly used for logistics and inventory management, their role in enhancing pedestrian safety remains limited. To address this gap, we integrate a walkability map, which assigns a score reflecting floor slipperiness to each area, into the robot's path planning. This enables the robot to perceive potential environmental hazards. By combining this walkability information with real-time pedestrian trajectory prediction, we enable robots to perform walkability-aware social navigation. Specifically, our approach employs Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) for online planning, which allows the robot to plan paths that can enhance human safety while keeping social compliance. We utilize MCTS's look-ahead capability by embedding the predicted future walkability scores into the reward function during the rollout process. This approach pushes the robot to select near-optimal actions that provide implicit guidance, thus guiding people away from low-walkability areas. Experiments in both simulation and real-world testing show improvement in pedestrian safety while maintaining real-time operational efficiency.

System overview. The pipeline integrates a SocialGAT Human Trajectory Predictor with an online MCTS Path Planner. The SocialGAT module provides the human baseline trajectory ($\mathbf{x}_{h_t}, \mathbf{y}_{h_t}$), which the Social Force Model uses to dynamically generate the influenced trajectory ($\mathbf{x}'_{h_t}, \mathbf{y}'_{h_t}$) during MCTS simulation. This influence accounts for the human reaction to the robot's simulated position ($\mathbf{x}_{r_t}, \mathbf{y}_{r_t}$) throughout the MCTS lookahead. Path evaluation uses the total reward calculated through a multi-objective reward, considering goal progress, collision avoidance, social comfort, walkability, and efficiency. (CAT: Concatenation; MLP: Multi Layer Perceptron)
